Currently the Supreme Court is hearing a case about gerrymandering in Wisconsin.
At the core of this case is the efficency gap.
The efficency gap measures wasted votes as a way to show partisan gerrymandering.
So what is a wasted vote?

It's a vote that if left out, would not effect the outcome. It's either a vote of a losing party, or a vote that was not needed by the winning party?
Say we have two districts and two parties/candidates in each.
This district has 8 pinks votes and 7 green votes. Pink wins by 1 vote.

This means that Pink did not waste any votes as they needed all of them to win, but Green wasted all 6 of their votes, as they did not win.
This district has 13 pinks votes and 2 green votes. Pink wins by 11 vote.

This means that Pink wasted 5 votes, as all they needed to win was 8, but Green only wasted 2 votes.
So what? There's no way to avoid wasted votes.

The key is when we look at them across all districts.

Wasted votes

0 votes 7 votes

Wasted votes

5 votes 2 votes
We then look at how these numbers add up across both districts. There are a total of 5 Pink votes wasted, which we divided by the total number of votes of 30, which gives us 17%.

There are a total of 6 Green votes wasted, giving us 20% of all votes.
The gives us a 3% pro Pink efficiency gap. Which is not bad.

The case says a 7% efficency gap or more would lead to one party always keeping control.
